Bringing in a professional is not admitting defeat — it's knowing the limits of your tooling and experience. I took my first Relay Lipstick to a luthier for final assembly and setup. That's a completely reasonable choice at any point in this process.

## When to call a luthier

**Neck attachment and alignment** is the most common point where a professional makes sense. If the neck isn't seating correctly or the string line isn't centered and you can't figure out why, a luthier can diagnose and correct it in an hour. Trying to force it yourself risks cracking the neck pocket.

**Nut slot work** is another one. Cutting nut slots too deep is irreversible. Luthiers do this dozens of times a week and have the tools for it.

**Fret leveling and crowning** — if the neck you received has uneven frets (which happens with budget necks), a fret level makes a significant playability difference. This requires specialized tools.

## What to tell them

When you bring a 3D-printed guitar body to a luthier, they may be skeptical. A few things worth communicating:

- The body is printed in PET-CF, which is dimensionally stable and hard
- The neck pocket is designed to spec — any fit issue is dimensional variation in the neck, not the body
- The electronics are [completed / need help with X]
- You want [a full setup / just the neck attachment / just nut work]

Having the [BOM](/relay/lipstick/bom) handy to show them the parts may be useful.

## What a full setup costs

A professional setup typically runs $60–$150 depending on your area and what's needed. For a first build, this is often worth it — you'll get a better-playing instrument and you'll learn from watching what they do.

## Finding a luthier

Local guitar shops that do repairs are the easiest starting point. Describe what you're bringing (3D-printed body, bolt-on neck, standard hardware) and ask if they're comfortable with it. Most will be — there's nothing exotic about the mechanical side of this build.

If you're having trouble finding someone, ask in the <DiscordLink /> — others in the community may know someone in your area.
