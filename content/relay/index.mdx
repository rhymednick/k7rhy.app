Relay is an electric guitar you print at home on a conventional 3D printer. Every model shares the same basic body shape. Each version changes the pickup layout and customizes the controls to match. The goal is a stable base you can trust for assembly and wiring, plus freedom to tune the look and sound afterward. I give you recommendations on pickups and controls and explain why I made those choices, but you should feel free to adapt the recommendations to your taste. If you're considering a change and want some feedback, come find me and other builders in our <DiscordLink>Discord community</DiscordLink> and ask us about it. 

These are not just the same guitar with different parts, each model is meant for a different musical focus. [Relay Lipstick](/docs/relay/lipstick) is the one you can build today with a full doc trail from files to setup. The others are still on the way. You can help decide what we finish next by voting in **Models in the lineup** below.

## What stays the same on every model

### Three pickups

I've always preferred the full-throated sound of a dual-humbucker guitar, but I never liked the idea of having to swap them out to change the character of the instrument. Therefore, I designed this body with room for three pickups to give you another dimension of flexibility. Relay uses three so you get more switch positions and blends. You can still aim for the thick, warm sound people expect from two wide pickups side by side. The platform just doesn't stop there. On each model the middle pickup has its own job. Sometimes it is a full third voice. Sometimes it mainly shapes the other two. If you prefer not to install the middle pickup, you can use the optional covers included with the models to fill that gap.

### 24.75 inch scale

**Scale length** is the distance from the bridge to the nut (the small bar at the top of the neck). It sets how long the vibrating part of each string is. Relay uses 24.75 inches. That is a little shorter than another common factory length (25.5 inches). Shorter strings are under less tension. They can feel a little easier to bend. The tone often sounds a little warmer than a longer scale guitar. I'm calling this out so that you understand that with this platform the scale length isn't something you can modify. If you use a different scale neck on these bodies, your guitar will never play in tune. 

That lower tension matters for a printed body. Plastic and glue do not like the same brute force as solid wood. A shorter scale asks less of the material.

Necks for this length are easy to find. Many suppliers stock them, so you spend less time hunting, have more variety to choose from, and often pay less.
