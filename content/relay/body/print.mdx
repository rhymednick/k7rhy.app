Getting the print right is the foundation everything else sits on — a warped body or delaminated joint will cause problems that are hard to fix later.

## Material

Print with **PET-CF (carbon fiber filled) or PET-GF (glass fiber filled)**. These materials are dimensionally stable under string tension and temperature variation in ways that PLA and PETG are not.

<DecisionNote variant="caution" title="Do not print with PLA or standard PETG">
    String tension creates sustained mechanical stress on the body, particularly around the neck pocket and bridge mount. PLA will creep over time — the neck angle will shift and the instrument will go out of intonation. PETG is better but still too flexible for the neck pocket area. PET-CF and PET-GF are what I've tested and can stand behind.
</DecisionNote>

The [Body overview](/relay/body) lists the filament with a purchase link. Two spools are required for a complete body.

## What you're printing

Three files, two materials:

- **Body.3mf** — the main body structure. Print in PET-CF or PET-GF.
- **Cap.3mf** — the front face that bonds to the body. Print in PET-CF or PET-GF.
- **Accessories.3mf** — small parts (pickup rings and similar). Standard PETG is fine here.

Body and Cap bond together into a single structure in the [Bonding](/relay/body/bonding) step. Print both before moving on — do not start bonding with only one part done.

## Print time

With one printer, expect about **50 hours of print time** for the complete body — plan for a week. Slower speeds improve layer adhesion with filled materials, so do not chase speed at the expense of quality here.

## Orientation and layer direction

Orientation matters more than it looks like it should. The neck pocket and bridge mount areas need to be printed so that layer lines run **perpendicular to the primary stress direction** — this reduces the chance of delamination under string tension. The .3mf files include pre-set orientations; do not rotate the parts unless you have a specific reason.

## Bed adhesion

PET-CF sticks well to textured PEI sheets. If you see warping at the corners of larger parts, use a brim. Do not use glue stick — it can cause uneven first layers that affect how parts fit together.

## What to watch for

- **Warping at the neck pocket:** This is the most structurally critical area. If the pocket warps, the neck won't seat squarely.
- **Layer delamination on thick walls:** Filled materials can delaminate if printed too fast. Reduce speed if you see separation.
- **Elephant foot at the first layer:** This will affect fit between parts. Use a brim if needed and sand the bottom layer flat before bonding.

## After printing

Let all parts fully cool — at least 24 hours off the bed — before checking fit. Check all mating surfaces for flatness before bonding. See [Bonding](/relay/body/bonding) for the bonding process.
